About the MMPI Test

The Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI) is a psychological test used to assess personality and detect psychopathology. It is used in clinical psychology, psychiatry, and human resources.

History of the Test

First published in 1943 by Stark Hathaway and J. Charnley McKinley, it consists of 566 statements. Responses: "Yes," "No," "Don’t know." It covers emotions, attitudes, and health. In the 1930s, psychological diagnostics faced significant challenges. Psychiatrists and psychologists of that time often relied on subjective methods, such as clinical interviews, which were unreliable and dependent on the specialist’s experience. There was no universal tool that could objectively assess personality traits and identify psychopathological conditions. In this context, Stark Hathaway, a clinical psychologist, and J. Charnley McKinley, a neuropsychiatrist, began working on a new test at the University of Minnesota. Their goal was ambitious: to create a standardized questionnaire that could not only diagnose psychiatric disorders but also account for individual personality traits. Unlike other tests of the time, which often focused on a single aspect (e.g., depression), the MMPI was designed to cover a wide range of psychological conditions. Hathaway and McKinley wanted the test to be empirical—based on data rather than theoretical assumptions.

IMPORTANT INFORMATION ABOUT THE MINNESOTA MULTIPHASIC PERSONALITY INVENTORY (MMPI)

The ideal time to complete the test is 90 minutes.

Read each question and decide whether it is TRUE or FALSE based on YOUR SITUATION. Your answers should be true or false depending on your circumstances. Remember that you are expressing your own opinion about yourself. Try to answer all questions if possible. Ensure that the number of unanswered questions does not exceed 10, otherwise the test will not be considered valid.

Important Information About the Test:
• This test was developed based on a unique synthesis of modern psychology and traditional human models, with its validity and reliability recognized worldwide.
• The test’s ability to reflect a person’s personality depends on the objectivity of its administration, the sincerity of the answers, and the individual’s level of self-awareness. Inconsistent answers may render the test invalid, resulting in an invalid profile assessment.
• The information obtained from this test serves as a map, illustrating a person’s attitudes, perception of life, inner world, and the dynamics underlying their behavior, but it is not the person themselves.
